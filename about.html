Research Agenda

My research develops macroeconomic theory and policy analysis for small open developing economies (SODEs). It focuses on how inflation, business cycles, public finance, and banking behaviour are shaped by foreign exchange constraint, imported-input dependence, fiscal dominance, partial dollarisation, and shallow financial markets.

Why this agenda?

Much of contemporary macroeconomics assumes that domestic marginal cost anchors inflation, that monetary policy has strong traction through expectations and interest rates, and that exchange rates mainly absorb shocks. In many small open developing economies, however, exchange-rate pass-through is rapid, imported capital and intermediate inputs play a central role in production, foreign exchange is often scarce, and banking systems absorb sovereign stress directly. Under these conditions, exchange rates often transmit shocks rather than absorb them, inflation can be driven by external and financial pressures, and standard measures of overheating, fiscal space, and policy effectiveness become unreliable.

Macro Toolkit for SODEs

Concept Purpose
FX Market Pressure A measure of macroeconomic stress that captures pressure in the foreign exchange market before it appears fully in conventional inflation indicators.
Fiscal Dominance Fiscal dominance refers to a situation in which the government’s financing needs constrain monetary policy and shape inflation, exchange-rate pressure, and financial stability.
Composite Output Gap/Overheating on Ice A measure of economic slack that combines the domestic output gap with an external output gap, allowing excess demand to be identified even when CPI inflation remains subdued. It captures what I call overheating on ice.
Open-Economy Golden Rule/Portfolio Golden Rule A framework for evaluating public investment in foreign-exchange-adjusted terms rather than relying only on standard fiscal rules or domestic return criteria. The Portfolio Golden Rule identifies a minimum share of FX-earning projects required for external sustainability.

Research Clusters

Public Finance for SODEs

This strand develops the theoretical, empirical, and policy implications of the Open-Economy Golden Rule of Public Finance. It asks how fiscal rules, project appraisal, and public investment should be redesigned for economies where foreign exchange is a binding macroeconomic constraint.

Business Cycles in SODEs

This line of research develops the ideas in Overheating on Ice. It studies how overheating is mis-measured when CPI inflation is a lagging indicator, how foreign exchange market pressure interacts with the business cycle, and how the composite output gap can improve macroeconomic analysis and policy design.

The Monetary Origins of Dutch Disease

This strand develops a monetary reinterpretation of Dutch disease. It studies how monetary financing, rather than resource-financed outlays alone, can generate Dutch disease effects. A central claim is that standard accounts neglect the supply-side implications of resource spending - what I call the Missing-Imports Problem. When monetary financing exceeds resource financing, Dutch disease is better understood as a money shock linked to fiscal dominance. This line of study also identifies the monetary diagnostics of resource booms and examines their implications for central bank independence, fiscal transparency, and public accountability.

Banking and Fiscal Dominance in SODEs

This research theme develops a banking-centred theory of fiscal dominance in small open developing economies. It studies how fiscal dominance reshapes banking behaviour, shortens the maturity structure of sovereign debt, affects interest rate spreads, alters risk pricing, and weakens financial stability in SODEs. It also examines the implications for central bank profitability and develops early-warning indicators of banking stress.

Overall, these research clusters contribute to a broader macroeconomics of small open developing economies. Their common premise is that inflation, business cycles, public finance, and financial stability must be analysed differently in economies where foreign exchange constraint, imported-input dependence, fiscal dominance, and banking balance sheets shape macroeconomic adjustment. The main goal is to develop concepts, diagnostics, and policy frameworks better suited to those realities.
