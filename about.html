Research Agenda

My research develops macroeconomic theory and policy analysis for small open developing economies. The central premise is that standard macroeconomic frameworks often rely on stylised facts drawn from advanced and financially integrated economies, and these travel poorly to economies where foreign exchange is scarce, imported inputs are central to production, banking systems dominate financial intermediation, and persistent fiscal dominance conditions macroeconomic adjustment.

In such settings, inflation, business cycles, public finance, banking behaviour, etc., cannot be understood adequately using standard frameworks alone. My work develops new concepts, empirical diagnostics, and policy tools for these economies.

Why this agenda?

Much of contemporary macroeconomics assumes that domestic marginal cost anchors inflation, that monetary policy has strong traction through expectations and interest rates, and that exchange rates mainly absorb shocks. In many small open developing economies, however, exchange-rate pass-through is rapid, imported capital and intermediate inputs play a central role in production, foreign exchange is often scarce, and banking systems absorb sovereign stress directly. Under these conditions, exchange rates often transmit shocks rather than absorb them, inflation can be driven by external and financial pressures, and standard measures of overheating, fiscal space, and policy effectiveness become unreliable.

Research Strands

Public Finance for SODEs

This strand develops the theoretical, empirical, and policy implications of the Open-Economy Golden Rule of Public Finance. It asks how fiscal rules, project appraisal, and public investment should be redesigned for economies where foreign exchange is a binding macroeconomic constraint.

Business Cycles in SODEs

This line of research develops the ideas in Overheating on Ice. It studies how overheating is mis-measured when CPI inflation is a lagging indicator, how foreign exchange market pressure interacts with the business cycle, and how the composite output gap can improve macroeconomic analysis and policy design.

The Monetary Origins of Dutch Disease

This strand develops a monetary reinterpretation of Dutch disease. It studies how monetary financing, rather than resource-financed outlays alone, can generate Dutch disease effects. A central claim is that standard accounts neglect the supply-side implications of resource spending, especially whether it expands non-resource imports. When monetary financing exceeds resource financing, Dutch disease is better understood as a money shock linked to fiscal dominance. This line of study also identifies the monetary diagnostics of resource booms and examines their implications for central bank independence, fiscal transparency, and public accountability.

Banking and Fiscal Dominance in SODEs

This research theme develops a banking-centred theory of fiscal dominance in small open developing economies. It studies how fiscal dominance reshapes banking behaviour, shortens the maturity structure of sovereign debt, alters risk pricing, and weakens financial stability in SODEs. It also examines the implications for central bank profitability and develops early-warning indicators of banking stress.
