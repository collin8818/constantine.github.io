New Structural Open Economy Macroeconomics

Modern macroeconomics—particularly the tradition of New Open Economy Macroeconomics (NOEM)—has been shaped largely by the experience of advanced, financially integrated small open economies. Its foundational stylised facts are well established: prices and wages are sticky; foreign exchange is abundant and freely traded; capital markets are deep and smooth cyclical fluctuations; and credible monetary policy anchors inflation expectations. In such environments, domestic capital overwhelmingly dominates total investment, inflation is primarily home-grown, and monetary policy transmits effectively through the interest-rate channel. External balance, in turn, is maintained through flexible exchange-rate adjustment with incomplete but predictable pass-through.

By contrast, the stylised facts that define developing and low-income open economies are fundamentally different. Prices are relatively flexible, and imported inflation transmits rapidly to domestic prices. Nominal exchange rates are fixed or tightly managed, so external imbalances adjust through movements in foreign reserves at the central bank. Fiscal dominance and shallow capital markets render the traditional interest-rate channel of monetary transmission largely ineffective, while financial intermediation remains predominantly bank-based. Domestic capital represents only a small fraction of the total capital stock, reflecting high import dependence for both consumption and investment goods.

In these economies, macroeconomic adjustment occurs primarily through quantity changes in the volume of bank credit, foreign exchange use and allocation, and fluctuations in real output rather than in prices. Therefore, inflation, nominal exchange rates, and interest rates are poor signals of underlying macroeconomic conditions and provide limited guidance for forward-looking expectations. This configuration defines a distinctive form of openness—not rooted in financial integration, but in import dependence and persistent foreign exchange scarcity.

Building on these empirical realities, my research develops a new analytical framework: New Structural Open Economy Macroeconomics (NSOEM), which replaces the analytical role of price rigidity with the availability of foreign exchange. The framework embeds the external sector, fiscal operations, and the banking system within a unified stock–flow accounting structure, offering a coherent explanation for the stylised facts that characterise developing and import-dependent economies.

New Structural Open Economy Macroeconomics advances this agenda by:

  • Updating the stylised facts that contemporary macro theory must explain;
  • Developing new tools, such as an FX-constrained output gap and an import-driven aggregate supply function;
  • Embedding financial intermediaries explicitly within the adjustment process;
  • Identifying threshold effects in policy effectiveness—for example, limits of inflation targeting under high import shares; and
  • Anchoring analysis in stock–flow consistent models that capture both the flow and accumulation of foreign assets and liabilities across sectors.

Precursors

Traditional Structuralist Macroeconomics long emphasised import dependence, foreign exchange constraints, and supply-side bottlenecks. Yet its focus on real-sector relationships often overlooked the roles of money, banking, and the stock dimensions of the external constraint, favouring flow-based analyses instead. The Monetary Approach to the Balance of Payments (MABP), by contrast, takes the opposite view—treating external imbalances as the result of monetary disequilibrium. However, money demand functions are notoriously unstable, and this line of research pays scant attention to the real sector and institutional structure of developing economies. Both traditions also remain detached from contemporary debates—such as the flattening of the Phillips Curve—or from modern modelling innovations grounded in microfoundations.

My work also shares analytical affinities with other traditions. Post-Keynesian and Kaleckian frameworks highlight demand-led growth, financial fragility, and income distribution, while recent research on dominant currency pricing underscores that international trade is largely invoiced in foreign currency. Each recognises the importance of external constraint, but none systematically integrates foreign exchange, banking, and fiscal operations within a stock–flow consistent framework. More importantly, they do not explore how the inadequate supply of foreign exchange reshapes core macroeconomic relationships—business cycle dynamics, output-gap measurement, inflation targeting, or the slope of the Phillips Curve.
