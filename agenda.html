New Structural Open Economy Macroeconomics

Research Agenda

My research develops macroeconomic theory and policy analysis for small open developing economies. The central premise is that standard macroeconomic frameworks often rely on stylised facts drawn from advanced and financially integrated economies, and these travel poorly to economies where foreign exchange is scarce, imported inputs are central to production, banking systems dominate financial intermediation, and fiscal dominance shapes macroeconomic adjustment.

In such settings, inflation, aggregate supply, business cycles, public finance, Dutch disease, and banking behaviour cannot be understood adequately using standard frameworks alone. My work develops new concepts, empirical diagnostics, and policy tools for these economies.

Why this agenda?

Much of contemporary macroeconomics assumes that domestic marginal cost anchors inflation, that monetary policy has strong traction through expectations and interest rates, and that exchange rates mainly absorb shocks. In many small open developing economies, however, exchange-rate pass-through is rapid, imported capital and intermediate inputs play a central role in production, foreign exchange is often scarce, and banking systems absorb sovereign stress directly. Under these conditions, exchange rates often transmit shocks rather than absorb them, inflation can be driven by external and financial pressures, and standard measures of overheating, fiscal space, and policy effectiveness become unreliable.
